1. Overview

Spontaneous Human Combustion (SHC) describes cases in which a human body is found severely burned—sometimes reduced largely to ash—while surrounding objects remain minimally damaged.

Public explanation: accidental ignition combined with the “wick effect.”
Internal classification: Localized Bio-Thermal Escalation Event.

Case reports consistently contain thermal inconsistencies that defy typical fire behavior modeling.



2. Scene Characteristics

Common forensic patterns include:

- Body largely incinerated, extremities sometimes intact
- Nearby furniture lightly scorched but not fully engulfed
- No clear external ignition source
- Limited fire spread beyond a confined radius

Temperatures required for such destruction typically exceed those achievable in normal residential fires without sustained fuel.



3. Historical Cluster References

Notable documentation regions:

- 18th century reports in France
- 19th–20th century cases in United Kingdom
- Modern forensic cases in the United States

Patterns appear sporadic with no consistent geographic concentration.



4. The Wick Effect Hypothesis

Mainstream fire science proposes:

- External ignition source (cigarette, spark).
- Clothing acts as wick.
- Body fat melts and sustains slow combustion.
- This can produce extended burn duration with limited flame spread.

However, certain archived cases report:

- No viable ignition source
- Victims located away from open flame
- Burn progression inconsistent with airflow patterns

Anecdotal accounts suggest:

No consistent medical predictor identified.
